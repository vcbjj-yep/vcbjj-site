Before You Begin

Who This
Is For

This programme is written for adults over 40 who train BJJ as a serious hobby — for health, personal growth, and the challenge of learning something difficult. You are not preparing for competition. You are preparing your body to learn without breaking.

If you feel stiff getting onto the mat, if your shoulders ache the morning after training, if you gas out halfway through a roll — this programme addresses those things directly.

Medical Clearance

If you have existing injuries, joint replacements, cardiovascular conditions, or haven't exercised in more than 6 months, get clearance from your doctor before starting.

How to Use This Programme

The programme runs 4 days per week, each session lasting 20–35 minutes. You can do it at home — a yoga mat, a resistance band, and a wall.

The Rule

If it hurts, stop. Discomfort is fine. Pain is a signal. There is an important difference between "this is hard" and "something is wrong."

Equipment

Required: Yoga mat, light resistance band (loop), wall space.
Optional: Tennis/lacrosse ball, foam roller, yoga block.

The Foundation

Five Pillars of
Physical Readiness

Every exercise maps to one or more of these pillars. They are the physical attributes that make BJJ technique possible. Click any pillar to expand.

1
SUPPLENESS
Restore softness and elasticity. Stiff bodies break.

Suppleness is not flexibility. Flexibility is a measurement. Suppleness is a quality — how soft, elastic, and resilient your tissues feel under load. A supple body absorbs force. A stiff body transfers it into joints.

Key areas for BJJ

  • Hip flexors and adductors (guard, shrimping)
  • Thoracic spine (rotational escapes)
  • Shoulders and neck (pressure from pins)
  • Ankles and wrists (base, posting)
2
STRUCTURE
Hold shapes under load. The strength that withstands pressure.

Structure is the ability to hold a position while someone tries to collapse it. Isometric resilience.

Muscle mass declines 3–8% per decade after 30. Isometric holds that mirror BJJ shapes are the intervention.

3
MOBILITY
Full, pain-free range of motion in every joint.

Mobility is flexibility plus control. Strong and stable at end range.

  • Hips: Cross-legged without pain? Squat below parallel?
  • Spine: Rotate 45° each way?
  • Shoulders: Hands behind back touch?
  • Ankles: Kneel without discomfort?
4
MOVEMENT
Foundational patterns that unlock grappling techniques.

The basic motor patterns BJJ is built on — the movement vocabulary that makes techniques possible.

  • Hip escape: Every escape and guard recovery
  • Bridge: Space from bottom mount/side control
  • Technical stand-up: Floor to standing safely
  • Crawling: Ground-based coordination
  • Rolling: Safe tumbling
5
CONDITIONING
An engine that survives training without crashing.

An energy system matching grappling: short bursts followed by active recovery.

  • Weeks 1–2: Aerobic base — controlled breathing during movement
  • Weeks 3–4: Work-rest intervals mirroring a 5-minute roll

Guidelines

Scaling &
Modifications

Can't do a movement at all

Reduce the range. Meet your body where it is.

Something causes pain

Stop. Substitute the closest pain-free alternative.

Programme feels too easy

Increase hold times 50%. Slow everything down.

Knee, shoulder, or back issues

Knees: reduce depth. Shoulders: below shoulder height. Backs: cat-cow, pelvic tilts, strict planks.

Guidelines

Recovery &
Listening

After 40, recovery is where adaptation happens.

Sleep

7–9 hours. Non-negotiable.

Hydration

Drink throughout the day. Dark urine = behind.

Soreness vs injury

DOMS = general ache, peaks 24–48h. Injury = sharp, joint-localised, worsens with movement.

Self-check

Before each session: "1–10, how ready?" Below 5 → mobility only. Below 3 → rest.

After Class

Post-Training
Reset

After an intense rolling session your nervous system is still in fight-or-flight mode — adrenaline, noradrenaline, and cortisol remain elevated even though training is over. In adults over 40, the parasympathetic "brake" is slower to engage and cortisol takes longer to clear. The result: you get home exhausted but lie awake for hours.

This 15-minute sequence switches your nervous system from sympathetic (alert, wired) back to parasympathetic (rest, recover). Do it immediately after class while your body is still warm, or at home before bed.

Why this works

These three exercises target different parasympathetic pathways — neurogenic tremoring (muscular discharge), vagus nerve stimulation (breathing mechanics), and passive muscular release (hip flexor and adductor unloading). Together they address the full fight-or-flight cascade, not just one piece of it.

Exercise 1 · 5 min
TRE Shaking Drill — Neurogenic Tremor Release
  • ∿∿
    EX-R1
    Trauma Release Exercise (TRE) shaking drill — lie on your back, soles of feet together, knees open. Lift your hips and brace your leg muscles hard. Hold for 2–3 minutes. Then lower your hips slowly and begin bringing your knees toward each other. At some point your legs will start to shake involuntarily — don't stop it. Let the tremor happen and move through your body. Stay with it for 2–3 minutes. Suppleness ▶ TRE Shaking Drill — Trauma Release Exercises 5 min total
How it works: The sustained contraction fatigues the psoas and deep hip flexors — the primary muscles that tense during fight-or-flight. When you release, the nervous system discharges residual tension through involuntary tremoring. This signals the brainstem that the threat has passed. Animals do this naturally after stress — they shake, reset, and return to baseline.
Exercise 2 · 5 min
Physiological Sigh Protocol — Vagus Nerve Activation
  • 🫁🫁
    EX-R2
    Physiological sigh breathing — lie on your back, one hand on chest, one on belly. Inhale through the nose in two distinct sniffs — the first fills the belly, the second tops up the chest. Then exhale slowly through the mouth for 6–8 seconds, letting the air drain completely. The exhale should be roughly twice the length of the inhale. Repeat continuously for 5 minutes. Eyes closed. Suppleness ▶ Physiological Sigh Breathing — Huberman Lab 5 min
How it works: The double inhale maximally inflates the alveoli in the lungs, and the extended exhale directly stimulates the vagus nerve — the primary parasympathetic "off switch." Research at Stanford showed this reduces cortisol faster than meditation or box breathing. Within minutes, heart rate variability shifts toward parasympathetic dominance.
Exercise 3 · 5–8 min
Supported Butterfly — Passive Hip Flexor & Adductor Release
  • ◇◇
    EX-R3
    Supported supine butterfly with diaphragmatic reset — lie on your back, soles of feet together, knees open (like the TRE starting position but completely passive). Place a pillow or rolled towel under each knee so the groin muscles are fully supported with zero effort. Arms rest at sides, palms facing up. Close your eyes. Breathe exclusively through the nose — 4-second inhale into the belly, 6-second exhale. Stay for 5–8 minutes. Suppleness ▶ Supta Baddha Konasana for Nervous System Recovery 5–8 min
How it works: This position passively opens the hip flexors and adductors — the muscles most loaded during guard work and shrimping — while the supported knees eliminate any muscular effort. The open-palm hand position is deliberate: clenched or pronated hands maintain sympathetic tone. Combined with slow nasal breathing, this creates a strong parasympathetic signal.
The sequence

Do all three in order: TRE shaking → Physiological sighs → Supported butterfly. The first exercise discharges muscular tension. The second switches the nervous system. The third holds you in the parasympathetic state long enough for it to stick. Total time: ~15 minutes. You should feel noticeably calmer by the end of the butterfly hold. If you only have time for one, do the physiological sighs — they're the fastest acting.

When to use this

After every evening class that includes sparring. Also useful after competitions, hard drilling sessions, or any time you feel "wired but tired" at bedtime.
