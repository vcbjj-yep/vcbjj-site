Session #128 — Guard Retention Games

CLA — Constraint-Based Guard Defense

Why this session sits where it does in your roadmap

Games 1–2: Single Limb (Leg then Arm)
You defend your hip line with one leg only, then one arm only. At your level this is not basic — it is exposing the difference between your instinctive movement and your trained movement. If there is a gap, it will show up here. The goal is perpendicular spine-to-hip movement, not pushing back.
Game 3: One Leg + One Arm
The coordination layer. This mirrors what your guard actually looks like mid-transition — one anchor committed, one tool free. This is the Game 3 version of the X-guard and arm drag entries you have been working: one thing is connected, one is free to steer.
Games 4–5: Staggered Legs + Knees to Chest
Staggered legs build the same geometry as your shin-to-shin and Reverse DLR setups. Knees to chest is the defensive shell that restarts everything — this is what you use when the partner has disrupted your guard and you need a clean re-entry point.
Game 6: Frame Recovery Under Pressure
Legs are pushed away, you add a frame arm to recover. At your level the focus is on whether the recovery is truly automatic or still deliberate. If you have to think about it, there is still work to do. The goal is zero cognitive load on retention so that all of your attention stays on the attack.
What to watch for today: You are being asked to do less — deliberately. At this stage in your training the constraint is the point. Every time you resist the urge to add a tool that was not allowed, you are training the quality of movement underneath your technique. That is what Phase 6 is built on.

Principles to Carry Into Every Session

Your Thinking Framework

When you posture up and give me space — that is not safety, that is an invitation.

Session #127: if your partner postures and creates space, use that space for joint attacks and chokes. The moment they think they are safe is the moment to attack. Stop treating space as a neutral event.

Pushing means you are defending. Pulling means you are attacking.

From Session #123. Check yourself in every position: which direction are you moving the relationship? If you are pushing more than pulling, you are losing the frame war even if nothing has gone wrong yet.

Skip the middle distance.

Session #127: use long-range guard and skip directly to close guard or underneath the partner for sweeps. The mid-range is where partners find their footing and pass. Do not linger there.

Anticipate the landing — not just the sweep.

Session #126: how your partner reacts mid-sweep determines where they land and which pin you take. Start reading this before the sweep completes. The top position is not the end of the sequence — it is the start of the next one.
