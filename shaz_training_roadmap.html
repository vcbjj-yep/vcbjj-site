Session #135 — De La Riva Guard

DLR — Three Connections, Dead Weight, Far Arm Sweep & Berimbolo

The guard that connects everything you have built in 2026

Why DLR Now
Every guard system you have worked in 2026 — shin-to-shin, Reverse DLR, butterfly, spider — has been building toward a full open guard architecture. DLR is the next node: it connects your steering principles (Phase 6) to your berimbolo and sweep entries in a single platform. You already understand connections and dead weight. This is those principles with a named structure around them.
Movement Prep — Hip Escapes and Inversions
The warm-up was not optional preparation — it was the physical vocabulary the whole session runs on. Hip escape fluency and comfort inverting are what make DLR sweeps and berimbolo feel controlled rather than scrambled. If those movements feel uncertain, the platform they support will also feel uncertain.
The Three-Point Connection
Foot on hip (outside hook) + sleeve or wrist grip + collar or cross-body grip. All three connect before you attack — this is the DLR rule. One or two connections is a partial platform. Three connections is a tilt machine. The dead-weight principle: once connected, sink your weight into your partner and remove their ability to step out before you begin the sweep.
Far Arm Sweep — Tilt, Not Pull
The sweep is generated by tilting your partner over their far side using the three-point connection. The far arm collapses first because their balance breaks — you are not pulling them over, you are removing the base they are standing on. This is the same principle as every other sweep in your system: move weight, not person.
Berimbolo Entry
From the DLR position, invert under your partner's hip line and use the rotation to take the back. The entry uses the same inversion mechanics from the warm-up. The rotation is not a scramble — it is a controlled movement powered by the three-point connection steering your partner's hips.

Principles to Carry Into Every Session

Your Thinking Framework

When you posture up and give me space — that is not safety, that is an invitation.

Session #127: if your partner postures and creates space, use that space for joint attacks and chokes. The moment they think they are safe is the moment to attack. Stop treating space as a neutral event.

Pushing means you are defending. Pulling means you are attacking.

From Session #123. Check yourself in every position: which direction are you moving the relationship? If you are pushing more than pulling, you are losing the frame war even if nothing has gone wrong yet.

Skip the middle distance.

Session #127: use long-range guard and skip directly to close guard or underneath the partner for sweeps. The mid-range is where partners find their footing and pass. Do not linger there.

Anticipate the landing — not just the sweep.

Session #126: how your partner reacts mid-sweep determines where they land and which pin you take. Start reading this before the sweep completes. The top position is not the end of the sequence — it is the start of the next one.

Losing a connection is finding the second door.

Session #134: the moment of disconnection is the moment to re-read and redirect — not to freeze. Familiarity leads to confidence leads to clarity. You are building this arc in your butterfly and DLR work now.

The guard leg steers — it does not hold.

Session #132: the direction you move your partner's weight is more important than the grip itself. DLR, Reverse DLR, shin-to-shin — the same steering principle runs through all of them. You are not restraining your partner; you are redirecting their base.
