PT Session — Reverse Scarf Hold Escapes & Crucifix Entry

Escaping reverse scarf hold — and turning defence into attack

The Problem We Started With
You are stuck in reverse scarf hold (kesa gatame facing away). The top player's constant threat is transitioning to mount. Your current default — double knees shrimping — slows them down but does not solve the position. Today we built four clean exits and one entry into a crucifix attack.
The Core Mechanics First
Use your near leg — drive from the hip, point your knee into your partner's hip to block the mount transition. The posting leg adjusts your hip angle and keeps the knee pinned to their hip. Keep your foot high and away from their grip at all times. This is the foundation every option below builds from.
Option 1 — Underhook Top Armpit → Crucifix
When your partner hand-fights to clear your leg post from their hip, they expose their triceps and elbow space. Underhook the top armpit in that moment and enter the crucifix. This is the same position Farrah worked in today — you drilled the receiving end of it, which gives you a clear picture of what the attacker needs and why the arm trap works.
Option 2 — Underhook Bottom Armpit → Seatbelt → Back Roll
Underhook the bottom armpit, get a seatbelt grip, pull their back to your chest, then take a backwards roll directly to the top pin. Clean and direct — minimal scramble required if the seatbelt is secure before you roll.
Option 3 — Underhook Hip → Belt Grip → Parallel Spine Sweep
Underhook their hip and connect both hands around their belt line. Walk away from them until your spine is parallel to theirs. Bridge, turn your head in the direction of the sweep, and come on top. Once there, maintain top pressure and walk to flatten them out for the pin.
Option 4 — High Reverse Scarf Already Established
If they have already settled in with a high pin, you cannot shrimp out immediately. Frame using your elbows and forearms, engage your lats and legs, bridge and walk upwards — this repositions their weight across your hip line. From there, choose one of the three options above.
Critical point: These four options must not be drilled in isolation. Always practise from the transition into reverse scarf — so you recognise how you ended up there, can work on preventing it, or can deliberately allow the pin knowing you have a predictable exit ready.

Principles to Carry Into Every Session

Your Thinking Framework

When you are playing guard: your job is to destabilise.

Get your partner's hips or hands to the mat. Everything else — sweeps, submissions, back takes — flows from that. If you are not destabilising, you are stalling.

A failed attack is not a failure — it is information.

Session #88 was entirely built around failed triangles. Every counter your partner makes tells you which follow-up to use. You have four mapped already. Use them.

Know how you got there — not just how to escape.

From today: every pin escape must be practised from the transition into that pin. If you only drill the escape, you will never recognise the moment you need it. Recognising the position as it forms is half the battle.

Connection and disconnection are your language.

Every grip, every frame, every hook is either building or breaking a connection. When you are pulling, you are attacking. When you are pushing, you are defending. Know which one you are doing at all times.
