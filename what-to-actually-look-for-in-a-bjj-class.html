What to Actually Look for in a BJJ Class

16 Jun 2026 By Vince Choo Getting Started Coaching

Most people walk into a BJJ class and spend the whole session trying to figure out whether they were good at it. That’s the wrong question — and it’s the reason so many adults walk out after one visit and don’t come back.

Whether you were good at it tells you almost nothing useful. You were a beginner. Of course you weren’t good at it. The more relevant question is whether the room gave you anything to work with — whether it was the kind of place where becoming good at it is actually possible.

Here’s what I’d actually be paying attention to.

Did anyone explain what just happened to you?

Not in a lecture. Not after the fact, in a debrief. In the moment — when you got tapped, when you ended up in a position you didn’t recognise, when you clearly had no idea what to do next — did anyone stop and say “here’s what was happening there”?

A lot of gyms operate on the theory that confusion is the lesson. That if you roll enough times in enough unfamiliar positions, the understanding will eventually show up on its own. For some people with a specific kind of patience, that’s probably true. For most adults who come to BJJ in their 40s with limited time and a body that doesn’t recover the way it used to, it’s just expensive confusion.

You are not a sponge waiting to absorb. You’re a thinking person who learns better when you have a framework to hang things on. A good BJJ room gives you that framework — not by over-explaining everything, but by making sure you leave with something you can actually think about before the next session.

Could you ask a question without feeling stupid for asking it?

This one is harder to evaluate in a single visit, but you can usually feel it. There’s a particular atmosphere in some gyms where questions are tolerated rather than welcomed — where the implicit social contract is that you get on with it, take your lumps, and figure it out. The culture is not explicitly hostile. It’s just not especially interested in you.

In a room that works for adult beginners, questions are normal. Not because the coach lowers the bar, but because the coach has thought about what the beginner experience is actually like, and has built the session around it. You shouldn’t feel like an interruption. You should feel like the room was, in some meaningful sense, expecting you.

If you found yourself staying quiet about things you didn’t understand because the vibe made that feel like the right call — that’s information. It means the room is not structured around your learning. It’s structured around something else: the experienced students, the competitive training, the general culture of get on with it. That might be fine for someone who already knows what they’re doing. It’s usually not fine for you.

Did the session feel like it had a pace — or just chaos?

Chaos in a BJJ session is not the same as intensity. Intensity is useful — it means the training is asking something real of you. Chaos is what happens when there’s no structure holding the session together: drills that don’t connect to anything, rolling that starts and stops with no clear logic, instructions that assume knowledge you don’t have yet.

A well-run session has a shape. There’s a reason the warm-up goes where it goes. There’s a connection between the technique you drilled and the position you’re expected to navigate when you roll. At the end of the hour, you might not be able to articulate exactly what you learned, but you’ll have a sense that the time was used well — that there was intention behind it.

That sense of intention is not a luxury. It’s the thing that makes the difference between training and just showing up. And in my experience, beginners can feel when it’s missing even when they don’t have language for it yet.

A few things that should make you walk straight back out

I want to be direct about a few specific things, because I’ve heard from people who experienced them and talked themselves into staying anyway.

Someone significantly bigger or more experienced going full intensity on you in your first session. This is not “how it is” in BJJ. It’s a cultural choice that some gyms make and others don’t. A beginner being subjected to sustained physical dominance by an experienced practitioner is not instruction — it’s just force. The room that does this is telling you something about how it thinks about beginners. Listen to it.

Pressure to sign up immediately. A gym that’s confident in what it offers doesn’t need to close you in the car park after the first class. If you feel pressured to commit before you’ve had a chance to think, or before you’ve visited more than once, that’s worth noting. Good gyms want people who actually want to be there, not people who signed something before they knew whether it was right for them.

Nobody knowing you were there. Not in a check-in sense — in a human sense. If you attended a session and left without anyone learning your name or asking anything about you, the room is not paying attention to its beginners. That might mean it’s too busy, or it might mean beginners are not particularly important to how the gym sees itself. Either way, it’s a signal.

What you’re actually evaluating

When you walk into a BJJ class for the first time, you’re not evaluating whether you have talent for this. You’re not deciding whether BJJ is right for you in some abstract, permanent sense. You’re evaluating this specific room, this specific coach, and whether the environment they’ve built is one where someone at your level, with your constraints, can actually learn.

That’s a much smaller and more answerable question — and it’s the right one to start with.

The room doesn’t have to be perfect. It doesn’t have to be exactly what you imagined. It just has to be honest about what it is, and genuinely interested in the person who just walked in the door.

If it has that, you’ll know. And if it doesn’t, you’ll know that too.
