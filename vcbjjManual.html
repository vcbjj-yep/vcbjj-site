The Foundation

Why We Train
This Way

BJJ at VCBJJ is treated like a functional skill set — not an identity, not a sport, not a lifestyle brand. The goal is the same as learning first aid: you want to know it, be able to use it under stress, and not need to think about it when it matters.

That framing changes everything about how training is designed. We are not preparing for competition. We are not collecting techniques. We are building a working understanding of how bodies control each other — and what to do in any situation you find yourself in.

The Guiding Principle

Technique is not the goal. The goal is to develop a response to any situation. Technique is how we name what worked — after you've already felt it.

How the System Works

Every session, every drill, every roll fits into a five-level map. The map is a loop — you can enter it at any point, and you'll cycle through all five levels repeatedly in any meaningful exchange. The levels don't describe how good you are. They describe what's happening in the moment.

Understanding the map means you always know where you are — and what your job is from that position.

Starting Out

Day 0 —
White Belt

This section is for students who are just beginning — or who want a clear picture of what the White Belt phase is actually building toward. Everything here maps directly to the Five Levels system you'll find in the next section.

What to Expect

◎
You will be taught from Day 0
No prior exposure to BJJ or grappling is assumed. Every concept, every position, every movement will be introduced and named. You won't be thrown in and expected to figure it out — but you will be expected to try things before you fully understand them. That's intentional.
◎
Sessions will feel different from other martial arts
There is no fixed warm-up routine. Warm-up responsibility sits with you, before class. Sessions move between partner drills, constraint games, and live rolling. You won't always know exactly what you're working on — that's not confusion, that's how skill develops in this system.
◎
"Rolling" is sparring — and it's part of the curriculum
Rolling means live, fully-resisting practice with a partner. It's not a test. It's not a competition. It's training — with real resistance, which is the only environment where technique actually sticks. You'll tap (submit) often when you're new. So does everyone. Tapping is how you learn safely.
◎
Overcome self-consciousness early
BJJ involves close physical contact that most adults haven't experienced since childhood. You'll be put in awkward positions, struggle, and not always know what to do. This passes faster than you think — and working through it is part of why the training builds real confidence.

The Self-Preservation Principle

BJJ at VCBJJ is rooted in a specific philosophy — one that predates the sport competition scene and is captured in the character 柔 (jū): yielding.

柔 — The Core Idea

Instead of imposing force on an attacker, you learn to receive their energy and redirect it. Strength is not the tool. Timing, position, and leverage are. A right move at the right time yields effortless results. A right move at the wrong time costs energy and produces frustration. A wrong move at the wrong time makes things worse.

This means the first priority at White Belt is not to attack — it's to survive, stabilise, and create options. Every defensive posture you learn in the early weeks is a direct expression of this principle.

The self-preservation objective, simplified: take your opponent off their feet → get to top → stay on top → submit or exit safely. If you have to play guard — keep them in your guard, don't let them pass, sweep or submit.

Fight IQ — The Bridge to the Five Levels

Before you encounter the Five Levels framework in the next section, here's the intuition behind it:

★
Give something to get something
Every position you attack from requires your partner to respond. Their response is what creates the next opportunity. You're not executing a fixed plan — you're reading reactions and selecting from what's available. This is why rolling never feels the same twice.
★
It's easier to move yourself than your opponent
Trying to move a resisting person is exhausting and inefficient. Moving your own body to a better position — even a small hip escape or a frame — changes the geometry of the exchange without requiring strength. This is the 柔 principle in practice.
★
Recognise conditions, then act
A right move at the right time is effortless. A right move at the wrong time costs energy you don't have. Training your ability to read conditions — when to hold, when to move, when to attack — is what separates developing grapplers from beginners. The Five Levels give you a map for this.
★
What you learn on the mat applies off it
How you approach pressure on the mats — whether you panic, freeze, or stay calm and make small moves — reflects how you approach difficult situations generally. Training BJJ builds more than physical skill. Most students notice the carry-over within a few months.
The System

Five Levels.
One Loop.

These five levels describe every possible situation in a grappling exchange. Click any level to expand it.

1
SURVIVAL
You're underneath someone. Your only job is to not get worse.

Level 1 is where everyone starts, and where everyone returns. If you're on the bottom of side control, mount, back control, or scarf hold — you are in a survival position. The situation is not neutral. Your partner has the advantage.

Your job at Level 1 is not to escape immediately. It's to prevent things from getting worse, buy time, and find the first opportunity to move. Most beginners make the mistake of burning energy trying to escape too fast, which is exactly how you get submitted.

What you're preventing

  • Partner getting chest-to-chest connection on you
  • Partner securing an underhook
  • Partner cross-facing your shoulder

What you're trying to achieve

  • Sit up — even just to your elbow is progress
  • Extend your body enough to get a leg or guard back
What "good at Level 1" looks like

You don't panic. You create small frames, move on the horizontal plane first, and wait for your partner to shift their weight before you attempt anything. You're calm in a bad spot — and that alone puts you ahead of most people.

2
GUARD ENGAGEMENT
You have a guard. Now you need to do something with it.

You've recovered your guard — close, open, K-Guard, half guard, seated, shin-to-shin. The exchange is now neutral, or close to it. Level 2 is about converting that neutral position into an attacking one.

There is only one primary directive at Level 2: Destabilize.

That means getting your partner's hand to the mat, or their hip to the mat. That's the signal. When their hand or hip touches down, the balance has shifted — and you enter the Funnel.

Guard types you'll work from

  • Close Guard — your legs wrapped around their waist; most controlled starting point
  • K-Guard / X-Guard — your legs inside theirs; high sweep and leg attack potential
  • DLR / Reverse DLR — hooking their leg; Gi-preferred
  • Shin-to-Shin — standing entry guard; works both formats
  • Open / Seated Guard — fluid; used when they're standing or passing

How destabilization works — the mechanics

Getting your partner's hands or hips to the mat isn't random — it's mechanical. The key is connecting to one of their legs, then using leverage to move their hips over their knees. Think of your connection as a crowbar: your grip is the fulcrum, and your body is the lever arm.

From open guard, your foot connects to your partner's leg in one of four compass directions — inside, outside, front, or behind. Each direction opens different sweep and destabilization paths. The grip you use on their foot or ankle matters: in the Gi, a pant cuff grip, a heel cup, or an underhook to your own shin each give different control. Your other foot manages their movement — blocking their ability to step somewhere unfavourable for you.

Two tools control your range: a long leg frame keeps distance when you need it, and shoelace hooks pull you back into grip range when you've been pushed away. Learning when to use each is the difference between maintaining your guard and losing the connection entirely.

The single question at Level 2

"Have I destabilized them?" If the answer is no — keep working on posture control and connection. If yes — you're entering Level 3.

3
THE FUNNEL
You're attacking. Three options. All connected. All cycling.

The Funnel is the most important concept in the system. Once you've destabilized your partner, you have three attack options — and they all feed into each other. This is not a decision tree. It's a cycle that your partner drives.

Here's what this looks like in practice: you attempt a sweep. Your partner bases their hand on the mat to stop it. That hand on the mat is now available for a kimura. They defend the kimura by straightening their arm. Straight arm is an armbar. The sweep didn't fail — it generated the next opportunity.

When you're stuck, ask yourself: "Am I sweeping, choking, or locking?" Then ask: "What did they just give me?" The second answer selects from the first.

The Sub-Funnels

Within each branch, there are chains — techniques that flow naturally from one to the next based on how your partner responds. You don't need to know all of these immediately. They emerge as you develop.

Why the Funnel works

Every defence your partner makes opens something else. You don't need a plan — you need to read what they give you. The Funnel is how you turn your partner's movement into your attack. The more your partner resists, the more options you have.

4
POSITION REVERSAL
Something worked. Now what?

A sweep landed. You're now on top, or back on your feet. This is the transition moment — and it's where many people lose what they just earned by not committing to the new position.

Your two options

  • Reverse the position — you're now on top, entering Level 5 (Top Control)
  • Recover to standing — enter the takedown game (throws, single legs, clinch)

The standing game feeds directly back into the system — a takedown lands, you end up in side control or mount (Level 5), and the loop continues. This is why the system is a cycle, not a checklist.

5
TOP CONTROL
You're on top. Control it — then attack, or start the loop again.

You're in a dominant position. The priority now is maintaining it — not scrambling for a submission before the control is established. This is where patience separates good grapplers from impatient ones.

The pinning principles

  • Chest-to-chest — your primary connection; if you lose this, you're losing control
  • Underhook control — trapping the near arm removes one of their main escape tools
  • Cross-face — limits their range of rotation; they must move hips before head
  • Balance and pressure — body weight into them, not propped up on your hands

From here, the system loops back. You'll enter the Funnel again from the top position — or your partner escapes and you return to Level 1 from the other side.

The cycle restarts

There is no endpoint. Every successful top control position is an opportunity to enter the Funnel again — now from the top. Your partner surviving and re-guarding sends them into their Level 2, which eventually puts you back in Level 1. The loop is the whole game.

Principles

The Rules That
Never Change

Regardless of position, format, or level — these six principles always apply. When something isn't working, the answer is almost always that one of these has been forgotten.

Push = Defend
When your hands are pushing, you're creating space — but not using it. Pushing is a defensive move, not an attacking one.
Pull = Attack
Pulling closes distance and creates leverage. If you want to attack, your hands need to be pulling, not pushing.
Hand or hip to mat
From guard, this is the signal to attack. The moment either one touches down, their balance has shifted — enter the Funnel.
Horizontal first
Escaping from the bottom? Move sideways before you try to sit up. Going vertical before horizontal is how escapes fail.
Inside space wins
Whoever controls the space between the two bodies controls the exchange. Most frame fights are really inside-space contests.
Make it meaningful
A grip only matters if it restricts or controls your partner's movement. Grabbing without purpose wastes energy and telegraphs your intentions.
Position Reference

Positions &
What to Do

Every position has one primary question to answer. Once you answer it, you know what to do next.

Close Guard
"Have I broken their posture?"
If no → break posture first, always
If yes → arm drag, kimura, sweep, triangle
They stand → K-Guard, lumberjack, star sweep
Side Control (bottom)
"Am I moving, or just waiting?"
Move before they settle — the window is brief
Horizontal escape first, then build height
Underhook → back take is your main attack
Mount (bottom)
"Are my elbows in?"
Elbows glued to ribs — protect the arms
Bridge into them (not away) to create lift
Opposite-hip shrimp → recover half guard
Back Control (bottom)
"Which side is safer to exit?"
Exit toward the choking arm side, not away
Hip escape down, trap a hook, create space
Don't chin-tuck and wait — keep moving
Half Guard (bottom)
"Do I have the underhook?"
No underhook → stay off your back, fight for it
Underhook → back take or sweep
Flattened → John Wayne sweep
Turtle
"Am I getting back to guard?"
Turtle is temporary — don't rest here
Granby roll → guard recovery
Being broken down → prevent by moving first
K-Guard
"Which way are they leaning?"
Forward → Matrix back take or X-Guard sweep
Stepping out → saddle / heel hook entry
Cross-face counter → spin under, dilemma
Scarf Hold (bottom)
"Can I connect my hook to my hand?"
Top hook → connect bottom hand to it → rotate
Underhook fight → Dagestani handcuffs
Bridge into elbow side, not away from it
Open Guard (bottom)
"Am I connected to a leg — and which direction?"
No connection → re-acquire grip on foot or ankle first
Outside connection → crowbar lever: move hips over knees
Use free foot to block their step options
Too far → shoelace hook to pull back into range
Too close → long leg frame to reset distance
How to use this reference

After a session, pick one position where you felt stuck and re-read it. Ask yourself which condition you were in, and whether you were answering the right primary question. Most problems trace back to missing step zero.

Technique Reference

Techniques
at a Glance

You don't need to memorise this list. These are the techniques you'll encounter most in training — use this to look up a name after a session, or to understand the context for something you've been drilling.

High Priority — Learn These First

Arm Drag
Guard → Back take
The highest-frequency technique in the system. 2-on-1 grip, pull across, hip escape to the same side. Every student trains this in some form.
Hip Escape (Shrimp)
Level 1 — all positions
The foundational movement. Every escape and recovery starts here. If nothing else is working, shrimp.
Triangle
Guard → Everywhere
The most-used submission in the system. One arm in, one arm out. Available from guard, mount, back, and arm drag entries.
Armbar
Guard / Mount / Back
Most-referenced technique overall. Multiple entries. The belly-down variant is your stack counter — rotate with your partner's direction, don't resist it.
Kimura
Guard / Half guard / Top
The Funnel's main control point. Use it to finish, sweep, or transition to armbar. If they straighten their arm, you already have the armbar.
Scissors Sweep
Close Guard
Sleeve grip + pants grip (Gi) or wrist grip (NoGi). Bottom leg scissors the thigh, top leg sickles the arm. Gi version amplified by pants grip.

Sweeps — Know the Options

Star Sweep
Close Guard (standing)
No grip required. Hook ankle, push hip. Clean format-agnostic sweep for when your partner stands.
Lumberjack Sweep
Close Guard (standing)
Both hands grab one ankle, both legs push the hip. Simple leverage — pair it with Star Sweep as two reads from the same situation.
Hip Bump
Close Guard
Sit up, post hand, drive hip into chest. When they block — kimura. When they duck — guillotine. The bump is the entry, not the finish.
John Wayne
Half Guard (flattened)
From flattened half guard bottom — use hip and leg to create space, re-secure underhook, roll your partner.
100% Sweep
Close Guard
Both legs on hips, rotate partner off their base. Identical mechanics Gi and NoGi. Often pairs with guillotine if they base forward.
Matrix
K-Guard
From K-Guard, weight forward — back take by rotating under and behind. Your first exit from K-Guard when they lean in.

Chokes — Context Matters

Arm Triangle
Mount / Side control
Works both formats. Head + arm trapped, hands connected, elbows point down. Space under the arm and around the neck is the entry condition.
Guillotine
Shoot defense / Guard
Actually easier NoGi. Arm-in variant gives mechanical advantage. Entry points: hip bump, half guard scramble, defending a single leg.
Cross Lapel
Close Guard / Mount GI
Three variations. If they block with "phonecall" defence → Baratoplata. The counter defence is the entry for the next attack.
Rear Naked Choke
Back Control
Top arm (choking arm) over, bottom arm under. Seat belt first, hooks second, choke when upper body is isolated. NoGi primary finish from back.
Bow & Arrow
Back Control GI
Collar grip + far pants grip. Extend like a bow. Near leg is the fulcrum, far leg is the post. Gi's most powerful back submission.
D'Arce / Canto
Top scramble
Both formats. Entry from top half guard or passing scrambles. The arm thread is the entry — if the arm is in, the choke is available.
Format Guide

Gi vs NoGi —
What Actually Changes

Training both formats doesn't mean learning two different systems. It means learning one system with two different gripping languages. The positions, the Funnel, the axioms — all the same. What changes is how you hold things.

The short version

Gi: More control available. Collar and sleeve grips create pauses and amplify every technique. The game is slower, more grip-dependent, and rewards patience.

NoGi: No grip anchor. Speed and body position replace textile control. The game is faster, more pressure-dependent, and rewards athleticism more.

The Translation — Same Concept, Different Grip

Concept Gi Version NoGi Version
Break posture Collar grip pull Head / neck control
Control the arm Sleeve grip Wrist / overhook
Guard retention Pants / belt grip Leg hooks, body contact
Back control Collar + hooks Seat belt emphasis
Sweep amplifier Pants / collar pull Pure hip mechanics
Best submission Bow & arrow (back), cross lapel (guard) RNC (back), guillotine (guard)

Training both formats accelerates development in both. The Gi teaches you grip discipline and patience. NoGi teaches you that you can't rely on friction — you have to earn every position with movement. Students who train both develop a more complete understanding faster than those who specialise early.

Off the Mat

Between
Sessions

Most of your development happens on the mat, under resistance. But there are things you can do between sessions that compound the work you're already doing.

✍
Write one thing down after every session
Not a technique list. One moment — what happened, what you tried, what your partner did. A single sentence is enough. Over weeks, patterns emerge that aren't visible session-to-session.
↺
Replay one position mentally
Pick one position you struggled with. Walk through the decision tree from that position — condition by condition. Not technique recall. "If they do X, I do Y." You're training decision speed, not muscle memory.
◎
Pick one Funnel question for your next session
Before you arrive, decide: "In this session I'm going to focus on the Kimura chain." Then try it regardless of whether it works. You're building a habit of intentional training, not reacting to whatever happens.
🔲
Use the self-assessment once a month
Not every week — progress is slow enough that weekly comparisons are discouraging. Monthly comparisons show real movement. Update the boxes honestly. The goal is accuracy, not optimism.
⟳
Don't skip survival work
When you drill at home or visualise — spend at least half your mental time in Level 1. Escapes and frames are the least glamorous part of the system and the most important. Everyone wants to drill the armbar. Nobody wants to drill the hip escape for the 200th time. The ones who do get better faster.
Understanding the Approach

Why We Train
This Way — FAQs

These are the questions that come up most often — usually after a session that felt different from what students expected from BJJ.

Why don't we drill techniques the way other gyms do?
Traditional technique drilling — watch a demonstration, repeat it 50 times — produces people who are good at drilling. What we want is people who can solve problems they haven't seen before, under resistance, in real time. The research on skill acquisition is clear: discovering a solution through experience, then having it named, produces better long-term retention and more adaptable skill than demonstration-first learning. The games and constraint-based training we use are designed to create that discovery. You'll feel less immediately competent — but you'll develop faster.
Why do the sessions sometimes feel chaotic or like I don't know what I'm doing?
That feeling is the learning. Cognitive effort during training — not knowing what to do, trying things, being wrong, trying again — is what creates durable skill. If every session feels clean and comfortable, you're probably not being challenged enough. The discomfort of "I don't know what to do here" is exactly when skill develops. Over time, those moments get shorter. That's progress.
I keep getting tapped by the same thing. Should I drill that escape more?
Sometimes yes — but usually the problem is one level earlier than where you're getting tapped. If you're being armbarred repeatedly, the question isn't "how do I escape armbars?" — it's "why is my partner getting to the armbar position in the first place?" Trace it back. Almost always it's a Level 1 or Level 2 problem, not a Level 3 one. Fix the position before fixing the submission.
Why do we train Gi and NoGi? Can I just do one?
You can specialise, but both formats will make you better at the other. Gi training builds grip discipline and teaches you to work in tight, controlled spaces. NoGi removes the crutch of textile control and forces you to move and connect with body position instead. The translation between formats isn't complicated — mostly grip substitutions — and the system is designed so that every concept you learn applies to both. Students who train both generally develop more complete understanding than those who only do one.
How do I know if I'm actually improving?
Use the self-assessment section in this guide monthly. More practically: track how long you survive in bad positions before the situation gets worse. Track how often you recognise what's available in the Funnel before your partner reacts. Track how many times you attempt something intentional vs just reacting. Progress in BJJ is slow and non-linear — there will be weeks where you feel like you're getting worse, which often means you've started trying more challenging things. That's not regression. That's development.
Why does the session sometimes feel like a game rather than technical training?
Because it is — and that's deliberate. Constraint games are how the system introduces new movement problems in a format that keeps both people engaged and produces emergent technique rather than copied technique. What feels like a game is actually a precisely designed learning environment. The game has a specific objective, specific constraints, and specific win conditions — all chosen to direct your attention toward the skill being developed. The technique you'd otherwise be shown in a demonstration is instead something you discover yourself, which means you'll remember it and be able to adapt it.
Should I be watching BJJ videos between sessions?
With caution. Watching high-level rolling can help you see the Funnel in action and understand what positions look like at speed. But technique videos — especially ones that teach isolated techniques out of position context — can actually slow down your development by giving you answers before you've experienced the problems. If you watch anything, watch positions and rolls, not technique breakdowns. And watch with one question in mind: "What's the Five-Level situation here? What is this person's Funnel?"
