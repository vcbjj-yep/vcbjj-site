The Foundation

Why We Train
This Way

BJJ at VCBJJ is treated like a functional skill set — not an identity, not a sport, not a lifestyle brand. The goal is the same as learning first aid: you want to know it, be able to use it under stress, and not need to think about it when it matters.

That framing changes everything about how training is designed. We are not preparing for competition. We are not collecting techniques. We are building a working understanding of how bodies control each other — and what to do in any situation you find yourself in.

The Guiding Principle

Technique is not the goal. The goal is to develop a response to any situation. Technique is how we name what worked — after you've already felt it.

How the System Works

Every session, every drill, every roll fits into a five-level map. The map is a loop — you can enter it at any point, and you'll cycle through all five levels repeatedly in any meaningful exchange. The levels don't describe how good you are. They describe what's happening in the moment.

Understanding the map means you always know where you are — and what your job is from that position.

Starting Out

Day 0 —
White Belt

This section is for students who are just beginning — or who want a clear picture of what the White Belt phase is actually building toward. Everything here maps directly to the Five Levels system you'll find in the next section.

What to Expect

◎
You will be taught from Day 0
No prior exposure to BJJ or grappling is assumed. Every concept, every position, every movement will be introduced and named. You won't be thrown in and expected to figure it out — but you will be expected to try things before you fully understand them. That's intentional.
◎
Sessions will feel different from other martial arts
There is no fixed warm-up routine. Warm-up responsibility sits with you, before class. Sessions move between partner drills, constraint games, and live rolling. You won't always know exactly what you're working on — that's not confusion, that's how skill develops in this system.
◎
"Rolling" is sparring — and it's part of the curriculum
Rolling means live, fully-resisting practice with a partner. It's not a test. It's not a competition. It's training — with real resistance, which is the only environment where technique actually sticks. You'll tap (submit) often when you're new. So does everyone. Tapping is how you learn safely.
◎
Overcome self-consciousness early
BJJ involves close physical contact that most adults haven't experienced since childhood. You'll be put in awkward positions, struggle, and not always know what to do. This passes faster than you think — and working through it is part of why the training builds real confidence.

The Self-Preservation Principle

BJJ at VCBJJ is rooted in a specific philosophy — one that predates the sport competition scene and is captured in the character 柔 (jū): yielding.

柔 — The Core Idea

Instead of imposing force on an attacker, you learn to receive their energy and redirect it. Strength is not the tool. Timing, position, and leverage are. A right move at the right time yields effortless results. A right move at the wrong time costs energy and produces frustration. A wrong move at the wrong time makes things worse.

This means the first priority at White Belt is not to attack — it's to survive, stabilise, and create options. Every defensive posture you learn in the early weeks is a direct expression of this principle.

The self-preservation objective, simplified: take your opponent off their feet → get to top → stay on top → submit or exit safely. If you have to play guard — keep them in your guard, don't let them pass, sweep or submit.

Fight IQ — The Bridge to the Five Levels

Before you encounter the Five Levels framework in the next section, here's the intuition behind it:

★
Give something to get something
Every position you attack from requires your partner to respond. Their response is what creates the next opportunity. You're not executing a fixed plan — you're reading reactions and selecting from what's available. This is why rolling never feels the same twice.
★
It's easier to move yourself than your opponent
Trying to move a resisting person is exhausting and inefficient. Moving your own body to a better position — even a small hip escape or a frame — changes the geometry of the exchange without requiring strength. This is the 柔 principle in practice.
★
Recognise conditions, then act
A right move at the right time is effortless. A right move at the wrong time costs energy you don't have. Training your ability to read conditions — when to hold, when to move, when to attack — is what separates developing grapplers from beginners. The Five Levels give you a map for this.
★
What you learn on the mat applies off it
How you approach pressure on the mats — whether you panic, freeze, or stay calm and make small moves — reflects how you approach difficult situations generally. Training BJJ builds more than physical skill. Most students notice the carry-over within a few months.
The System

Five Levels.
One Loop.

These five levels describe every possible situation in a grappling exchange. Click any level to expand it.

1
SURVIVAL
You're underneath someone. Your only job is to not get worse.

Level 1 is where everyone starts, and where everyone returns. If you're on the bottom of side control, mount, back control, or scarf hold — you are in a survival position. The situation is not neutral. Your partner has the advantage.

Your job at Level 1 is not to escape immediately. It's to prevent things from getting worse, buy time, and find the first opportunity to move. Most beginners make the mistake of burning energy trying to escape too fast, which is exactly how you get submitted.

What you're preventing

  • Partner getting chest-to-chest connection on you
  • Partner securing an underhook
  • Partner cross-facing your shoulder

What you're trying to achieve

  • Sit up — even just to your elbow is progress
  • Extend your body enough to get a leg or guard back
What "good at Level 1" looks like

You don't panic. You create small frames, move on the horizontal plane first, and wait for your partner to shift their weight before you attempt anything. You're calm in a bad spot — and that alone puts you ahead of most people.

2
GUARD ENGAGEMENT
You have a guard. Now you need to do something with it.

You've recovered your guard — close, open, K-Guard, half guard, seated, shin-to-shin. The exchange is now neutral, or close to it. Level 2 is about converting that neutral position into an attacking one.

There is only one primary directive at Level 2: Destabilize.

That means getting your partner's hand to the mat, or their hip to the mat. That's the signal. When their hand or hip touches down, the balance has shifted — and you enter the Funnel.

Guard types you'll work from

  • Close Guard — your legs wrapped around their waist; most controlled starting point
  • K-Guard / X-Guard — your legs inside theirs; high sweep and leg attack potential
  • DLR / Reverse DLR — hooking their leg; Gi-preferred
  • Shin-to-Shin — standing entry guard; works both formats
  • Open / Seated Guard — fluid; used when they're standing or passing
The single question at Level 2

"Have I destabilized them?" If the answer is no — keep working on posture control and connection. If yes — you're entering Level 3.

3
THE FUNNEL
You're attacking. Three options. All connected. All cycling.

The Funnel is the most important concept in the system. Once you've destabilized your partner, you have three attack options — and they all feed into each other. This is not a decision tree. It's a cycle that your partner drives.

Here's what this looks like in practice: you attempt a sweep. Your partner bases their hand on the mat to stop it. That hand on the mat is now available for a kimura. They defend the kimura by straightening their arm. Straight arm is an armbar. The sweep didn't fail — it generated the next opportunity.

When you're stuck, ask yourself: "Am I sweeping, choking, or locking?" Then ask: "What did they just give me?" The second answer selects from the first.

The Sub-Funnels

Within each branch, there are chains — techniques that flow naturally from one to the next based on how your partner responds. You don't need to know all of these immediately. They emerge as you develop.

Chain A — Arm
Omoplata ↓ if they roll to escape Triangle ↓ if they posture up Armbar
Chain B — Kimura
Kimura ↓ if they straighten arm Guillotine ↓ if they defend neck Sweep
Chain C — Collar GI
Cross lapel choke ↓ phonecall block Baratoplata ↓ they defend Re-enter Funnel
Why the Funnel works

Every defence your partner makes opens something else. You don't need a plan — you need to read what they give you. The Funnel is how you turn your partner's movement into your attack. The more your partner resists, the more options you have.

4
POSITION REVERSAL
Something worked. Now what?

A sweep landed. You're now on top, or back on your feet. This is the transition moment — and it's where many people lose what they just earned by not committing to the new position.

Your two options

  • Reverse the position — you're now on top, entering Level 5 (Top Control)
  • Recover to standing — enter the takedown game (throws, single legs, clinch)

The standing game feeds directly back into the system — a takedown lands, you end up in side control or mount (Level 5), and the loop continues. This is why the system is a cycle, not a checklist.

5
TOP CONTROL
You're on top. Control it — then attack, or start the loop again.

You're in a dominant position. The priority now is maintaining it — not scrambling for a submission before the control is established. This is where patience separates good grapplers from impatient ones.

The pinning principles

  • Chest-to-chest — your primary connection; if you lose this, you're losing control
  • Underhook control — trapping the near arm removes one of their main escape tools
  • Cross-face — limits their range of rotation; they must move hips before head
  • Balance and pressure — body weight into them, not propped up on your hands

From here, the system loops back. You'll enter the Funnel again from the top position — or your partner escapes and you return to Level 1 from the other side.

The cycle restarts

There is no endpoint. Every successful top control position is an opportunity to enter the Funnel again — now from the top. Your partner surviving and re-guarding sends them into their Level 2, which eventually puts you back in Level 1. The loop is the whole game.

Principles

The Rules That
Never Change

Regardless of position, format, or level — these six principles always apply. When something isn't working, the answer is almost always that one of these has been forgotten.

Push = Defend
When your hands are pushing, you're creating space — but not using it. Pushing is a defensive move, not an attacking one.
Pull = Attack
Pulling closes distance and creates leverage. If you want to attack, your hands need to be pulling, not pushing.
Hand or hip to mat
From guard, this is the signal to attack. The moment either one touches down, their balance has shifted — enter the Funnel.
Horizontal first
Escaping from the bottom? Move sideways before you try to sit up. Going vertical before horizontal is how escapes fail.
Inside space wins
Whoever controls the space between the two bodies controls the exchange. Most frame fights are really inside-space contests.
Make it meaningful
A grip only matters if it restricts or controls your partner's movement. Grabbing without purpose wastes energy and telegraphs your intentions.
Position Reference

Positions &
What to Do

Every position has one primary question to answer. Once you answer it, you know what to do next.

Close Guard
"Have I broken their posture?"
If no → break posture first, always
If yes → arm drag, kimura, sweep, triangle
They stand → K-Guard, lumberjack, star sweep
Side Control (bottom)
"Am I moving, or just waiting?"
Move before they settle — the window is brief
Horizontal escape first, then build height
Underhook → back take is your main attack
Mount (bottom)
"Are my elbows in?"
Elbows glued to ribs — protect the arms
Bridge into them (not away) to create lift
Opposite-hip shrimp → recover half guard
Back Control (bottom)
"Which side is safer to exit?"
Exit toward the choking arm side, not away
Hip escape down, trap a hook, create space
Don't chin-tuck and wait — keep moving
Half Guard (bottom)
"Do I have the underhook?"
No underhook → stay off your back, fight for it
Underhook → back take or sweep
Flattened → John Wayne sweep
Turtle
"Am I getting back to guard?"
Turtle is temporary — don't rest here
Granby roll → guard recovery
Being broken down → prevent by moving first
K-Guard
"Which way are they leaning?"
Forward → Matrix back take or X-Guard sweep
Stepping out → saddle / heel hook entry
Cross-face counter → spin under, dilemma
Scarf Hold (bottom)
"Can I connect my hook to my hand?"
Top hook → connect bottom hand to it → rotate
Underhook fight → Dagestani handcuffs
Bridge into elbow side, not away from it
How to use this reference

After a session, pick one position where you felt stuck and re-read it. Ask yourself which condition you were in, and whether you were answering the right primary question. Most problems trace back to missing step zero.

Technique Reference

Techniques
at a Glance

You don't need to memorise this list. These are the techniques you'll encounter most in training — use this to look up a name after a session, or to understand the context for something you've been drilling.

High Priority — Learn These First

Arm Drag
Guard → Back take
The highest-frequency technique in the system. 2-on-1 grip, pull across, hip escape to the same side. Every student trains this in some form.
Hip Escape (Shrimp)
Level 1 — all positions
The foundational movement. Every escape and recovery starts here. If nothing else is working, shrimp.
Triangle
Guard → Everywhere
The most-used submission in the system. One arm in, one arm out. Available from guard, mount, back, and arm drag entries.
Armbar
Guard / Mount / Back
Most-referenced technique overall. Multiple entries. The belly-down variant is your stack counter — rotate with your partner's direction, don't resist it.
Kimura
Guard / Half guard / Top
The Funnel's main control point. Use it to finish, sweep, or transition to armbar. If they straighten their arm, you already have the armbar.
Scissors Sweep
Close Guard
Sleeve grip + pants grip (Gi) or wrist grip (NoGi). Bottom leg scissors the thigh, top leg sickles the arm. Gi version amplified by pants grip.

Sweeps — Know the Options

Star Sweep
Close Guard (standing)
No grip required. Hook ankle, push hip. Clean format-agnostic sweep for when your partner stands.
Lumberjack Sweep
Close Guard (standing)
Both hands grab one ankle, both legs push the hip. Simple leverage — pair it with Star Sweep as two reads from the same situation.
Hip Bump
Close Guard
Sit up, post hand, drive hip into chest. When they block — kimura. When they duck — guillotine. The bump is the entry, not the finish.
John Wayne
Half Guard (flattened)
From flattened half guard bottom — use hip and leg to create space, re-secure underhook, roll your partner.
100% Sweep
Close Guard
Both legs on hips, rotate partner off their base. Identical mechanics Gi and NoGi. Often pairs with guillotine if they base forward.
Matrix
K-Guard
From K-Guard, weight forward — back take by rotating under and behind. Your first exit from K-Guard when they lean in.

Chokes — Context Matters

Arm Triangle
Mount / Side control
Works both formats. Head + arm trapped, hands connected, elbows point down. Space under the arm and around the neck is the entry condition.
Guillotine
Shoot defense / Guard
Actually easier NoGi. Arm-in variant gives mechanical advantage. Entry points: hip bump, half guard scramble, defending a single leg.
Cross Lapel
Close Guard / Mount GI
Three variations. If they block with "phonecall" defence → Baratoplata. The counter defence is the entry for the next attack.
Rear Naked Choke
Back Control
Top arm (choking arm) over, bottom arm under. Seat belt first, hooks second, choke when upper body is isolated. NoGi primary finish from back.
Bow & Arrow
Back Control GI
Collar grip + far pants grip. Extend like a bow. Near leg is the fulcrum, far leg is the post. Gi's most powerful back submission.
D'Arce / Canto
Top scramble
Both formats. Entry from top half guard or passing scrambles. The arm thread is the entry — if the arm is in, the choke is available.
Format Guide

Gi vs NoGi —
What Actually Changes

Training both formats doesn't mean learning two different systems. It means learning one system with two different gripping languages. The positions, the Funnel, the axioms — all the same. What changes is how you hold things.

The short version

Gi: More control available. Collar and sleeve grips create pauses and amplify every technique. The game is slower, more grip-dependent, and rewards patience.

NoGi: No grip anchor. Speed and body position replace textile control. The game is faster, more pressure-dependent, and rewards athleticism more.

The Translation — Same Concept, Different Grip

Concept Gi Version NoGi Version
Break posture Collar grip pull Head / neck control
Control the arm Sleeve grip Wrist / overhook
Guard retention Pants / belt grip Leg hooks, body contact
Back control Collar + hooks Seat belt emphasis
Sweep amplifier Pants / collar pull Pure hip mechanics
Best submission Bow & arrow (back), cross lapel (guard) RNC (back), guillotine (guard)

Training both formats accelerates development in both. The Gi teaches you grip discipline and patience. NoGi teaches you that you can't rely on friction — you have to earn every position with movement. Students who train both develop a more complete understanding faster than those who specialise early.

Off the Mat

Between
Sessions

Most of your development happens on the mat, under resistance. But there are things you can do between sessions that compound the work you're already doing.

✍
Write one thing down after every session
Not a technique list. One moment — what happened, what you tried, what your partner did. A single sentence is enough. Over weeks, patterns emerge that aren't visible session-to-session.
↺
Replay one position mentally
Pick one position you struggled with. Walk through the decision tree from that position — condition by condition. Not technique recall. "If they do X, I do Y." You're training decision speed, not muscle memory.
◎
Pick one Funnel question for your next session
Before you arrive, decide: "In this session I'm going to focus on the Kimura chain." Then try it regardless of whether it works. You're building a habit of intentional training, not reacting to whatever happens.
🔲
Use the self-assessment once a month
Not every week — progress is slow enough that weekly comparisons are discouraging. Monthly comparisons show real movement. Update the boxes honestly. The goal is accuracy, not optimism.
⟳
Don't skip survival work
When you drill at home or visualise — spend at least half your mental time in Level 1. Escapes and frames are the least glamorous part of the system and the most important. Everyone wants to drill the armbar. Nobody wants to drill the hip escape for the 200th time. The ones who do get better faster.
Your Development

When Progress
Stalls

After coaching hundreds of grapplers, the ones who never improve have one thing in common.

None of them had a feedback loop.

They watched Gordon Ryan, John Danaher, and every other big name they could find. They studied match footage. They bought privates. They sparred more. But without a feedback loop, you cannot see what you cannot see. They chased novelty instead of building a process for understanding where their mistakes were actually coming from. Adding more technique to a foundational problem is like slapping more paint on a cracked brick wall. So they never improved consistently over a long period of time — and then they plateaued fast.

If this resonates, you need to start implementing a feedback loop. You need a process for when you make mistakes — and more importantly, how you recognise those mistakes in the first place.

The core problem

Most students evaluate their training by outcome — did I tap, or did I get tapped? That's the wrong question. Outcome tells you the score. A feedback loop tells you why the score is what it is — and what to change. Without it, more mat time just means more repetition of the same errors.

First: Are You Actually Plateauing?

Before you diagnose a plateau, you need to check whether what you're feeling is real. Progress in BJJ is slow and often invisible — especially to the person experiencing it. A lot of what gets labelled as a plateau is actually progress that hasn't been noticed yet, because the student has no process for noticing it.

Ask yourself whether any of the following is true:

This is not a plateau — this is progress

You're surviving longer in bad positions. The tap still comes — but later. The time between the mistake and the submission is growing. That gap is your development.

You're failing more because you're attempting more. If you're getting caught doing things you wouldn't have tried six months ago, that's not regression. That's the feedback loop working.

The chaos feels familiar. You can't yet solve the problems you're facing — but you recognise them. You know where you are even when you can't get out. That recognition is a major shift. It's where skill starts.

Your training partners are harder than they used to be. If the difficulty of your rolls has increased, results naturally decline. That's calibration, not regression.

A genuine plateau is when none of the above are true: same partners, same context, nothing shifting in any direction — and you have no clarity about why. That's worth investigating. That's where the feedback loop comes in.

The Four Types of Plateau

If you've confirmed it's a real plateau, the next step is identifying which type. They feel identical from the inside — flat, frustrating, directionless — but they have completely different causes. Applying the wrong fix to the wrong type is the most common reason students stay stuck.

Type 1
The Same Loop, Repeated

You have a game that works well enough that you keep using it — and because it keeps working, you never need to grow past it. This is the most comfortable plateau to be in and the hardest one to notice, because things don't feel stuck. You're not losing. You're not struggling. But you're also not developing.

What it looks like: You have two or three things you rely on. They work against the partners you usually roll with. You rarely feel genuinely challenged.

What moves this: Deliberately constrain yourself. Give your usual options away — roll without using your best position, without attacking your favourite submission. If you can't function without your two go-to moves, those moves are doing your thinking for you. The goal is a game that survives when the obvious path is closed.
Type 2
Knowing Without Feeling

You can explain the technique. You understand the concept. But when rolling, it disappears. You revert to what's automatic. This gap between understanding and action is one of the most common and most frustrating experiences in BJJ.

What it looks like: You know exactly what you should have done — five seconds after the moment has passed. You can describe the correct response, but your body doesn't produce it under pressure.

What moves this: The gap closes through repetition with intent, not more information. Choose one concept — a single position, a single entry — and make it the only question you're asking in your next three sessions. Don't try to execute it perfectly. Try to find the situation it applies to, and attempt it every single time it appears, regardless of outcome. Volume of attempt matters more than success rate at this stage.
Type 3
Collecting Without Connecting

You're training consistently and absorbing material — but the pieces aren't joining up into a game. You have techniques without a context for them. When a situation arises, you freeze because you have too many options and no reliable way to choose between them.

What it looks like: Training feels productive. Knowledge is growing. But in live rolling, decision time is slow and you regularly feel overwhelmed. You find yourself thinking rather than moving.

What moves this: Step back from accumulating and start connecting. Take the Funnel framework and map what you already know onto it — not what you want to learn, but what you have. Pick one sweep, one choke, one lock you already own, and commit to running those three as a chain. A small connected game is more useful than a large disconnected library.
Type 4
The Invisible Foundation

Progress in the more advanced parts of your game has stalled because something earlier in the pyramid is shaky — and it's invisible because it's not where your attention is. You're working on Level 3 and Level 4 material, but Level 1 or Level 2 still has gaps that undercut everything built on top of it.

What it looks like: You attempt attacks, but rarely from a stable position. You get close to finishing submissions but regularly lose the position before you can complete them. You feel like the technique is right, but it keeps not working.

What moves this: Use the self-assessment in this guide honestly. Work on the lowest level where anything feels uncertain. The instinct is to keep working at the exciting end — but almost every advanced technique problem traces back to a survival or connection problem earlier in the system. Fix the floor, and the ceiling rises by itself.

The Feedback Loop

This is what the students who never plateau have that the others don't. It's not talent. It's not more training hours. It's a repeatable process that turns every session — good or bad — into information they can use.

The loop has five stages. When it runs well, you develop naturally. When it breaks at any stage, you stop developing — regardless of how much time you put in. Most students who plateau aren't failing at the mat work. They're failing at the loop.

1
Experience

Something happens on the mat. You attempt something, you get caught, you survive a position, you make a move instinctively. This is raw material — it only becomes learning if you do something with it.

2
Notice

You pay attention to what happened. Not just the result — but the moment before it. What did your partner do? What did your body do? What made the situation what it was? This is where most students drop the thread — the session ends, and the experience evaporates.

BROKEN LOOP SIGNAL: You can't recall a single specific moment from your last session. The whole session exists as a vague impression of how it generally went.
3
Reflect

You turn the moment into a question. Not "what technique should I have used?" — but "what was actually happening in that position? What was the position asking for?" This is where coaching, this manual, and the Funnel framework are most useful — as a lens to make sense of what you experienced, not as instructions to follow.

BROKEN LOOP SIGNAL: You reflect on what you should have done but can't connect it to what your body actually did. The answer exists in theory but not in experience.
4
Set One Intention

From the reflection, you carry one specific, testable question into your next session. Not "I'll try to be better at guard." One thing: "When my partner stands up, I'm going to immediately look for the inside space." This gives your attention somewhere to go during the fog of live rolling.

BROKEN LOOP SIGNAL: You arrive at every session with no particular focus. Whatever happens, happens. Sessions feel productive but not directional.
5
Try Again — With the New Question

You return to the mat. The intention from Stage 4 shapes what you notice in Stage 2. The loop restarts — now with slightly more experience, slightly more data. Progress is happening, even when the result of any individual roll looks identical to last week.

↺   LOOPS BACK TO STAGE 1
The loop doesn't require perfection at any stage. You don't need to notice everything — just one thing. You don't need a sophisticated reflection — just one honest question. The intention doesn't need to work — just be specific enough to give your attention a direction. Small inputs into each stage compound over months into genuine development.

A Simple Way to Run the Loop

You don't need a journal or a system. Three questions, after every session, kept as brief as you like:

Question 1
What happened once that I want to understand?

One moment. Not a summary of the session — a specific moment. If nothing comes to mind, that's the signal that you need to start paying closer attention on the mat.

Question 2
What was the position asking for that I didn't give it?

Not "what technique should I have used?" — but what was the situation? Was it a survival moment? A connection problem? A timing issue? Use the Five Levels and the Funnel as your map.

Question 3
What's my one question for next time?

A single, specific, testable intention. "Next session I'm going to try to stay perpendicular every time someone tries to pass." That's the loop completing. That's what makes the next session different from the last one.

When to Bring It to Your Coach

If you've been running the loop sincerely for three or four sessions and nothing is shifting — not more success, but not even more clarity about what's happening — bring that to a session. Not "I feel stuck," but: "This is the moment I keep experiencing, this is what I think is happening, and this is what I've tried." That conversation will be far more productive than any amount of general technique drilling.

Understanding the Approach

Why We Train
This Way — FAQs

These are the questions that come up most often — usually after a session that felt different from what students expected from BJJ.

Why don't we drill techniques the way other gyms do?
Traditional technique drilling — watch a demonstration, repeat it 50 times — produces people who are good at drilling. What we want is people who can solve problems they haven't seen before, under resistance, in real time. The research on skill acquisition is clear: discovering a solution through experience, then having it named, produces better long-term retention and more adaptable skill than demonstration-first learning. The games and constraint-based training we use are designed to create that discovery. You'll feel less immediately competent — but you'll develop faster.
Why do the sessions sometimes feel chaotic or like I don't know what I'm doing?
That feeling is the learning. Cognitive effort during training — not knowing what to do, trying things, being wrong, trying again — is what creates durable skill. If every session feels clean and comfortable, you're probably not being challenged enough. The discomfort of "I don't know what to do here" is exactly when skill develops. Over time, those moments get shorter. That's progress.
I keep getting tapped by the same thing. Should I drill that escape more?
Sometimes yes — but usually the problem is one level earlier than where you're getting tapped. If you're being armbarred repeatedly, the question isn't "how do I escape armbars?" — it's "why is my partner getting to the armbar position in the first place?" Trace it back. Almost always it's a Level 1 or Level 2 problem, not a Level 3 one. Fix the position before fixing the submission.
Why do we train Gi and NoGi? Can I just do one?
You can specialise, but both formats will make you better at the other. Gi training builds grip discipline and teaches you to work in tight, controlled spaces. NoGi removes the crutch of textile control and forces you to move and connect with body position instead. The translation between formats isn't complicated — mostly grip substitutions — and the system is designed so that every concept you learn applies to both. Students who train both generally develop more complete understanding than those who only do one.
How do I know if I'm actually improving?
Use the self-assessment section in this guide monthly. More practically: track how long you survive in bad positions before the situation gets worse. Track how often you recognise what's available in the Funnel before your partner reacts. Track how many times you attempt something intentional vs just reacting. Progress in BJJ is slow and non-linear — there will be weeks where you feel like you're getting worse, which often means you've started trying more challenging things. That's not regression. That's development.
Why does the session sometimes feel like a game rather than technical training?
Because it is — and that's deliberate. Constraint games are how the system introduces new movement problems in a format that keeps both people engaged and produces emergent technique rather than copied technique. What feels like a game is actually a precisely designed learning environment. The game has a specific objective, specific constraints, and specific win conditions — all chosen to direct your attention toward the skill being developed. The technique you'd otherwise be shown in a demonstration is instead something you discover yourself, which means you'll remember it and be able to adapt it.
Should I be watching BJJ videos between sessions?
With caution. Watching high-level rolling can help you see the Funnel in action and understand what positions look like at speed. But technique videos — especially ones that teach isolated techniques out of position context — can actually slow down your development by giving you answers before you've experienced the problems. If you watch anything, watch positions and rolls, not technique breakdowns. And watch with one question in mind: "What's the Five-Level situation here? What is this person's Funnel?"

Crucifix
Entry & Attacks

The crucifix is one of the most versatile pinning and attacking positions in BJJ. This page covers how to enter it from the double-under stack pass, how to lock it in securely, and what you can do once your partner is trapped there.

Entry
Technique Library · Crucifix · Part 1
Double-Under Stack Pass → Crucifix
Gi & NoGi Passing
Where does this come from? You've passed your partner's legs and taken a double-under grip — both arms threading under both their thighs. From here, instead of completing a standard stack pass, you redirect the energy into a crucifix position. The key is that their hips go up and over their own shoulders.
The Entry Sequence
  1. 1
    Connect your hips to their lap
    Before anything else, your hips need to be pressing down into your partner's lap — not just your hands. This is the anchor point. If this connection is loose, the whole pass loses its force.
    Feel: pressure through your whole pelvis, not just your arms
  2. 2
    Ratchet your torso up — chest to sacrum
    Walk your upper body upward until your chest is pressing into your partner's sacrum (the base of their spine). You are essentially wrapping around them. Think of it as stacking them rather than pushing them.
    Landmark: your chest connects to their sacrum, not their lower back
  3. 3
    Drive their hips over their shoulders
    The downward drive from your chest tips their hips up and over. When this works, their knees are heading toward the mat behind their own head — they are in a highly compressed position with very limited power.
    Target: partner's knees touch or approach the mat behind them
  4. 4
    Set your legs to trap and enter crucifix
    This is the most technical step — see the leg configuration section below. One leg secures their near arm; the other hooks under their far leg. Done correctly, this creates a wedge that makes escape very difficult.
    Both legs are doing different jobs simultaneously
Leg Configuration
The crucifix is only as secure as your leg arrangement. Both legs are active and doing different jobs. Getting this right is the difference between a solid pin and your partner simply rolling out.
Far leg (your leg furthest from their head)
Hooks over their near arm
Your far leg goes over and traps your partner's near arm against their body. This prevents them from posting or framing with that arm.
Near leg (your leg closest to their head)
Hooks under their far leg
Your near leg reaches under and hooks their far leg. This stops them from turning into you or using their legs to create space and roll out.

Together, these two hooks create a wedge — one controlling the upper body and one controlling the lower body. Your partner cannot move effectively in any direction without breaking one, and breaking one usually gives the other more leverage.

How They Will Try to Escape

Most partners will try one of three things. Knowing these in advance means you can stay ahead of the reaction:

Escape attempt 1
Slide down
They try to wriggle their hips downward, out of compression. Counter: keep your downward hip drive consistent. Any gap you give them is the space they'll use.
Escape attempt 2
Free the arm
They try to pull the trapped arm out. Counter: your leg configuration should make this very hard — if they're freeing an arm easily, check your far leg position.
Escape attempt 3 — most common
Turtle toward the head
They try to tuck their chin and roll forward (toward where their head is), hoping to come up to a turtle position. Counter: follow the roll and keep your chest connection. Don't let them get space between your chest and their back.
◈
Coach's note The hardest part of this entry isn't strength — it's the sequence. Many people try to set their legs before they've properly stacked the partner, and then wonder why it doesn't hold. Stack first, then legs. The compression is what creates the opportunity for the leg configuration to work.

Why so many options? The crucifix controls both of your partner's arms and immobilises their body. That leaves a lot of attacking surface available. The two most common submissions — neck crank and straight arm lock — are your default go-to. The triangles and leg-only arm locks are available when your partner defends or adjusts, giving you follow-up options without having to reset.

Escaping
Reverse Scarf Hold

Reverse scarf hold (kesa gatame facing away) is a pin that many people ignore in training — until they get stuck in it. This page covers why it's awkward, how to stop it getting worse, and four routes out of it.

Survival
Technique Library · Pins · Bottom
Reverse Scarf Hold — Escape Options
Gi & NoGi Bottom Position
Why is this position tricky? In reverse scarf hold, your partner is beside you but facing your feet. Their weight is across your upper body. The instinct is to push them off — but pushing rarely works here because their weight distribution makes that easy for them to absorb. The solution is hip and knee management, not strength.
First — Stop the Situation Getting Worse
Anti-mount defence — do this before attempting any escape
🦵
Use your near knee, driven from the hip. Point your near knee across your body toward your partner's hip. This blocks them from sliding into mount. The knee movement must come from the hip — not just bending the knee in place.
📐
Use your posting leg to adjust your hip angle. Your far leg posts on the mat and allows you to shift your hips left or right — keep this mobile so you can continuously point your near knee at your partner's hip as they move.
🙅
Keep your posting foot high and out of reach. If your partner can grab your foot, they can straighten your leg and remove the post. Foot high, heel up.
Your Four Escape Options

Each option opens because of something your partner does. You're not forcing these — you're reading their movement and taking what's offered. Tap each option to expand the steps.

1
Crucifix Set-Up via Top Armpit
▶
  • Your partner hand-fights to clear your posting leg from their hip — this exposes their triceps and the space near their elbow.
  • Take an underhook into their top armpit (the armpit facing the ceiling, away from the mat).
  • Use this underhook to begin entering a crucifix — the same position covered in the section above.
  • The hand-fighting that was meant to solve their problem has actually created yours.
◈
Coach's note This is the direct connection between Daryl's session and Farrah's session on the same day. The crucifix is not just a top position — it's also an escape route from the bottom.
2
Seatbelt Grip + Backwards Roll to Top Pin
▶
  • Underhook your partner's bottom armpit — the one nearest the mat.
  • Reach your other arm over their shoulder or neck to create a seatbelt grip (one arm under, one arm over).
  • Pull their back to your chest — you want chest-to-back contact before you move.
  • Perform a backwards roll, taking them with you. You end up on top.
  • Maintain the top pressure as you land and work to establish your pin.
3
Hip Walk + Bridge Sweep
▶
  • Underhook your partner's hip — reach your arm around their hip closest to your head.
  • Connect both hands around their belt line (or waist in NoGi).
  • Walk your hips away from them — keep walking until your spine is roughly parallel to theirs. This takes time and small steps; don't rush it.
  • Bridge upward and turn your head in the direction you're sweeping — your body follows your head in a bridge.
  • Once on top, keep your pressure down and walk yourself forward to flatten them out for the pin.
◈
Coach's note The hip walk is where most people give up too early. You need to keep walking until the spines are genuinely parallel — that's when the bridge becomes mechanical and powerful rather than just muscular.
4
Frame + Bridge When They've Already Established a High Pin
▶
  • If your partner has already settled their weight high across your upper chest — don't panic and push. Build a frame first.
  • Use your elbows and forearms to create a firm structure against their body — not stiff arms, but bent, load-bearing frames.
  • Engage your lats (the muscles on the sides of your back) and your legs — this connects your frame to your whole body.
  • Bridge — push the mat away with your legs — and walk your hips upward until your partner's body is sitting across your hip line, not your chest.
  • From here, look for Options 1, 2, or 3 above. You've reset the situation into something more workable.
The bigger picture — practise in context
🔗
Don't drill these in isolation. The value of escape training comes from recognising the transition that put you in reverse scarf hold in the first place. When you know how you ended up there, you have three choices: prevent it early, allow it on purpose (because you know you can escape), or start the escape immediately before they settle.
🔄
Start from the preceding position when you drill. If reverse scarf hold usually appears after a failed guard recovery, start your drill from the guard recovery attempt. Practise the full sequence, not just the escape. This builds timing, not just technique.
Before You Train

Joint Warm-Up — Controlled Articular Rotations

This is how you prepare your joints before class. It takes about eight to ten minutes and it changes what you're able to do once rolling starts. Think of it as the difference between starting a cold engine and a warm one.

What CARs are. Controlled Articular Rotations (CARs) come from the Functional Range Systems approach developed by Dr. Andreo Spina. The principle is straightforward: every joint in your body has a range of motion it can and should move through. CARs ask you to move each joint slowly and deliberately through its full available range — under muscular control, not momentum. You're not swinging your arms to loosen up. You're actively driving the joint to its limit and holding it there briefly before continuing the rotation. The slowness is the point.

Done correctly, CARs do three things at once. They prepare joint tissue for load. They give you an honest read of where your body is today — stiffness, restriction, discomfort that wasn't there last week. And they maintain the range you have, which matters more as you get older and less forgiving if you stop moving.

Why BJJ specifically needs this

BJJ puts unusual demands on specific joints in specific ways. Your hips rotate in almost every position you'll ever be in — guard, passing, takedowns, escapes. Your shoulders get loaded from extreme angles when submissions are threatened. Your knees flex and rotate simultaneously under body weight. Your neck works against resistance when your partner is chest to chest with you.

An honest warm-up matches those demands. General movement and a light jog gets your heart rate up but does not specifically prepare a shoulder to resist a kimura, or a knee to manage the rotation required for a proper DLR hook. CARs do. Each rotation you'll do below maps directly to something that happens in class.

Note on pain. CARs should be pain-free throughout. If a joint is painful to rotate, reduce the range to where you're comfortable, do what you can, and mention it to your coach. Discomfort from a cold joint warming up is different from pain — you'll learn to distinguish the two over time. When in doubt, reduce range and slow down further.

The Routine — Eight to Ten Minutes

Do this sequence in order. Stand still for the standing joints; seated or on your back for the ones that need a stable base. Move slowly — a single rotation should take five to eight seconds. You are not stretching; you are driving the joint actively to its end range.

JOINT 1 · CERVICAL SPINE
Neck
Both
BJJ connection: Your neck is under constant directional load in any chest-to-chest position. Top player cross-faces, bottom player bridges. The rotation component matters whenever your partner is driving into your collar or you're working from turtle.
  1. 1
    Starting position
    Stand tall. Feet hip-width. Shoulders relaxed and down — not hunched. Fix your gaze on a point ahead of you at eye level.
  2. 2
    Flexion — chin toward chest
    Slowly lower your chin toward your sternum. Drive as far as control allows without rounding your upper back. Hold the end point for one full second.
  3. 3
    Lateral — ear toward shoulder
    Continue the rotation: from chin-down, bring your left ear toward your left shoulder. Keep your shoulder down — don't let it rise to meet your ear. The work is in the neck, not the shoulder.
  4. 4
    Extension — eyes to ceiling
    Continue rotating your head back until you're looking at the ceiling. Only go as far as is comfortable — the cervical spine extension range varies significantly between individuals. Never force this.
  5. 5
    Complete the circle
    Continue to the right ear toward right shoulder, then back to start. That's one repetition. Do three full circles in each direction. Reverse the direction for the second set.
    ⟳ 3 reps each direction · 5–8 sec per rotation
JOINT 2 · GLENOHUMERAL
Shoulder
Both
BJJ connection: Kimuras, Americanas, armbars, and collar chokes all put your shoulder through end-range positions under resistance. The shoulder CAR is arguably the most important one in this sequence.
  1. 1
    Starting position
    Stand tall. Keep both sides of your ribcage even — do not let the working shoulder drag the ribcage with it. Think of the ribcage as fixed; only the arm and shoulder move. This is the hardest part to get right, and the most important.
  2. 2
    Arm floats forward and up
    Let the arm rise forward (flexion) and continue up toward the ceiling. Keep the elbow soft. As the arm rises above shoulder height, begin to internally rotate the arm so that your thumb points behind you at the top of the circle.
  3. 3
    Continue behind the body
    Sweep the arm back and down behind your body in extension. You will feel how far the arm can actually travel behind you — most people discover this is less than they assumed. Don't force it; stay within your available range.
  4. 4
    Return and reverse
    Complete the circle back to the starting position. Do three reps forward, then three reps in reverse direction. Repeat for the other arm. Each arm takes about 90 seconds.
    ⟳ 3 reps each direction, each arm · ribcage stays still throughout
JOINT 3 · HIP
Hip
Both
BJJ connection: Everything in guard work is a hip problem. DLR hooks, inversions, berimbolo, bridge-and-roll escapes, triangle setups — all require controlled hip rotation under load. This CAR is your direct preparation for those movements.
  1. 1
    Starting position
    Stand on one foot. Use a wall, post, or your partner's shoulder for light balance support — this is not cheating, it lets you focus entirely on the moving hip. Keep your standing knee soft, not locked.
  2. 2
    Bring the knee forward and up
    Lift the free knee toward your chest (hip flexion). As the knee rises, begin to rotate it outward — opening the hip — so that the knee sweeps out to the side at the top of the movement. Think of drawing a large circle in the air with your knee.
  3. 3
    Continue to extension
    Sweep the leg back and behind you (hip extension), keeping the pelvis as still as possible. The movement is in the femur rotating in the socket — not in the lower back arching. If your lower back is working hard, your range of hip extension has been reached.
  4. 4
    Return and reverse
    Complete the circle. Do three reps in each direction per hip. Move slowly enough that you can feel where the range changes — those are the edges worth exploring. Swap legs and repeat.
    ⟳ 3 reps each direction, each hip · pelvis stays level throughout
JOINT 4 · TIBIOFEMORAL / PATELLOFEMORAL
Knee
Both
BJJ connection: The knee doesn't rotate in the same free way the hip does — it flexes and extends, with a small controlled rotation available at the end of flexion. Knee injuries are among the most common in BJJ. This CAR addresses the rotational loading the knee experiences during guard work, ashi-garami positions, and takedowns.
  1. 1
    Seated starting position
    Sit on a chair or mat with the thigh supported. The knee hangs freely off the edge. Alternatively, stand on one leg and allow the free knee to hang flexed at 90 degrees.
  2. 2
    Full flexion
    Slowly pull the heel toward your seat — maximum knee flexion. At end range, briefly isometrically contract the hamstring to reinforce the position. Hold for two seconds.
  3. 3
    Tibial rotation
    While keeping the knee flexed near end range, gently rotate the lower leg inward and then outward. This is the small rotational component of the knee — you're not forcing a large movement, just exploring and reinforcing what's available.
  4. 4
    Full extension
    Slowly straighten the knee to full extension. At end range, contract the quadriceps isometrically. Hold two seconds. Then return to flexion. Do five slow full cycles per knee — the range exploration is the goal, not speed.
    ↕ 5 reps each knee · no forced rotation beyond comfortable range
JOINT 5 · TALOCRURAL
Ankle
Both
BJJ connection: Ankle mobility is required for base stability in standing positions, for foot hooks in DLR and shin-to-shin guard, and for managing the load through your lower leg during takedown attempts and ground scrambles.
  1. 1
    Seated or standing
    Cross one ankle over the opposite knee. Wrap your hand gently around the forefoot for support — this is to keep the foot steady, not to force movement.
  2. 2
    Slow circumduction
    Drive the foot slowly through its full circle — pointing the toes, then sweeping through eversion (sole facing away), then dorsiflexion (toes pulling back), then inversion. Keep the movement coming from the ankle, not by rotating the whole lower leg.
  3. 3
    Both directions
    Do five circles in each direction per ankle. Make the circle as large as available range allows — not bigger.
    ⟳ 5 reps each direction, each ankle
JOINT 6 · THORACIC SPINE
Upper Back — Rotation
Both
BJJ connection: The thoracic spine (mid-back) is where rotation comes from in every clinch, every sprawl, every time you angle off during passing or framing. When thoracic rotation is restricted, the lower back compensates — and lower back strain is the result. Opening thoracic rotation keeps load where it belongs.
  1. 1
    Seated or half-kneeling
    Sit on the mat with legs crossed, or take a half-kneeling position. Place your hands lightly at your temples — elbows wide. This position locks out the lumbar spine and isolates the movement to the thoracic region.
  2. 2
    Rotate slowly left
    Without letting the hips rotate with you, rotate your upper body to the left as far as available range allows. Your pelvis should stay facing forward throughout. At end range, add a small isometric — gently try to rotate further without actually moving. Hold two seconds.
  3. 3
    Rotate slowly right
    Return through centre and rotate to the right with the same attention. Do five slow rotations each side. This is also a useful assessment tool — asymmetry between left and right is worth noting.
    ↻ 5 reps each side · hips fixed, thoracic only
JOINT 7 · RADIOCARPAL / DISTAL RADIOULNAR
Wrist
Both
BJJ connection: Wrist grip-fighting, posting, posting during a scramble, and resisting wrist locks all load the wrist in multiple planes. Gi training adds the sustained gripping load of sleeve and collar work. Finger and wrist health often deteriorates quietly — this CAR keeps you tracking it.
  1. 1
    Forearms forward, elbows at sides
    Extend both arms forward with a light fist. Keep the elbows close to your body or rest your forearms on your thighs.
  2. 2
    Circumduction
    Slowly circle the wrist through full circumduction — flexion, ulnar deviation, extension, radial deviation. Make the circle deliberate and slow. Go around five times each direction per wrist.
  3. 3
    Pronation / Supination
    Separately: with the elbow fixed at your side, slowly rotate the forearm from palm-down to palm-up (supination) and back. This is the radio-ulnar rotation component. Do five slow reps each direction.
    ⟳ 5 reps each direction, each wrist

At a Glance

The full sequence, in order:

Neck → Shoulder (right, then left) → Hip (right, then left) → Knee (right, then left) → Ankle (right, then left) → Thoracic spine → Wrist (right, then left)

Allow eight to ten minutes. Do not rush through the early joints to get to the later ones — each joint gets the same quality of attention. If you only have five minutes, prioritise hips, knees, and shoulders in that order. Those are the joints that carry the highest load during class.
Three things CARs are not
✗
They are not stretching. Stretching asks the tissue to lengthen passively. CARs ask the joint to move actively through its available range under muscular control. The distinction matters — passive flexibility and active control are different qualities, and BJJ requires control.
✗
They are not cardio warm-up. Your heart rate will not rise significantly during CARs. That's fine. Joint tissue preparation and cardiovascular preparation are separate things. CARs do the joint preparation; the first few minutes of drilling do the cardio part.
✗
They are not optional once you're over 35. Recovery time increases with age, and the joints that get the least attention during a warm-up are the ones that accumulate wear most visibly over years of training. Ten minutes before class is a reliable investment.
Positional Thinking

Position Webs —
What's Available Right Now

Every position you land in is a junction, not a dead end. From any given spot there are usually four or five different things available — sweeps, submissions, escapes, transitions — all accessible depending on where your partner is and what they're doing.

The problem that most students hit is treating positions like a one-way street: "I tried the sweep, it didn't work, now I'm stuck." The position hasn't failed. You've just looked down one branch and not seen the others.

The key shift

Stop asking: "What is the next technique in my sequence?"
Start asking: "What is my partner giving me right now?"

Your partner's reaction — the direction they lean, which hand they base with, which leg they step — selects your option. The position is the same. What changes is which branch is ripe.

The diagrams below show three positions you'll regularly find yourself in. Each one has multiple exits. Read the web before you move — it takes half a second and changes everything.

Technique Chain
Knee Shield Half Guard — The Funnel in Action GI & NOGI
(bottom player — proactive sequence)

The position web on the previous page gives you the options. This section shows how to string them into a chain where every defence your partner chooses becomes the entry to the next attack. You don't wait for openings — you create them by threatening something real.

The core idea

Most people look for an opening before they move. The problem is your partner is doing the same thing — so you're both waiting, and whoever reacts first is already one step behind. Instead: push the pace, force a reaction, use that reaction as your entry. The funnel is a chain of initiated threats, not a menu of responses.

Step 1 — Open the underhook without telegraphing

From knee shield half guard, your first objective is the underhook. Don't reach for it directly. Your partner knows it's coming and will fight it before you get there. Instead, use the knee shield to threaten the tilt sweep — make their hips the problem. As they post their hand and shift weight to defend the sweep, the space for the underhook appears as a byproduct. Shoot it then, not before.

Key point: Three connection points established (underhook, near knee, foot position) always beat one or two. The more connected you are, the stronger the platform and the more options you hold.

Step 2 — Back take attempt; read the defence

With the underhook in, attempt the back take. Most partners will anticipate this and respond immediately. Two common counters, both of which you want:

A
They whizzer (overhook your underhook)
They insert their arm over your underhooking arm to block the back take and square up.
Use it. Control their whizzering wrist, push that arm toward their own belly, and underhook their near knee (protecting yourself against an accidental self-Americana). Sweep to top position.

Timing detail: During the sweep there is a moment when both of their knees point upward. Extract your trapped leg at that exact moment. Miss it and you land in their half guard instead of on top.
B
They post hard on their far arm
They extend their far arm strongly to the mat to resist being taken to the back, rather than using the whizzer.
Don't grip-fight the post. Use it as a prop. Elevate your hips off it and move them high, bringing your knee over their shoulder to enter the Choi Bar. The harder they post, the better the leverage you have.

Step 3 — Low knee-tap variation

From the underhook position, a low knee-tap takedown attempt will draw a posting response. Rather than abandoning the attack, use the underhook to hang your weight off your partner and keep pressure on the posting arm. Force them to further commit their weight through the post. Once they are committed and loaded, transition into a leg triangle. The key is continuous side pressure — it keeps them committed to that posting arm long enough for your leg to be freed and swing behind their neck.

Octopus Guard Chain

The Octopus guard entry (reaching your far arm through their near leg from half guard bottom) can reach a hip bump sweep. When it works, it works cleanly. When it doesn't, here is what to expect:

1
Partner cross-faces with their free arm
They reach their free arm across your face to disrupt the Octopus entry.
Anticipate this — it is the most common counter. Don't fight to remove the arm. They have already given it to you. Use the knee shield to create space, reach your top hand over their shoulder, connect to a Gable grip, and establish an arm saddle on that arm. Extend away and keep their elbow off the mat to sweep.

If they defend the sweep, transition directly to a Choi Bar on the same arm. The cross-face defence has now been funnelled into a submission.
The thread running through all of this: you are never looking for a single technique to land cleanly. You are creating a problem your partner must solve, then using the solution they choose as your next entry. That is a funnel — not a reaction sequence, but a chain of initiated threats that removes their options one by one.

5
Hip Bump Sweep
When: their weight is stacked forward and they're heavy on your chest
Sit up fast, underhook one arm, and drive your hip into them as you rotate. Works best when they commit weight downward. Often sets up the kimura if they base — so it's the same two-step as the scissor sweep, just from a different weight commitment.
How to practise this

Pick one position. Land in it during rolling. Before you do anything, pause for half a second and ask: which of the five is available right now? You don't need to plan the whole sequence — just identify which branch your partner is giving you. That's the habit.

Over time you'll start reading it before you even land in the position. That's when the rolling feels different — less reactive, more like you're offering options and waiting for them to choose.

From Exploration to Your Game

Building Your Personal Game —
Exploration, Sparring, Competition

There is a gap between learning a technique and actually owning it. This section is about how you cross that gap — and what the process looks like at each stage.

Exploration — Mapping the Unknown

Before you can build a game, you need to know what your body does and what your partner does in response. The way to learn this is not to study more technique — it is to explore with deliberate curiosity.

Try moving one body part at a time: your left leg. Your right leg. Your right arm. Your shoulder. Your head. Your hip. Lean your torso away, then into your partner. Each movement is a probe — a small experiment. What you are looking for is not a technique that works; you are looking for a reaction. What does your partner do when you move your hip this way? What changes when you push with your shoulder instead of your arm?

What this is actually doing

Every body part you move into unfamiliar territory is mapping a space you did not know before. Each reaction your partner gives is data. Over time, the unfamiliar becomes familiar. You are not learning moves — you are learning the landscape.

This is why the same partner drilled over many sessions reveals more than a hundred different partners drilled once. The map accumulates. You start to know what this person — and by extension, many people — will do in a given moment before they do it.

From Reactions to Your Game

At some point in your exploration, you will notice something: certain movements of yours produce the same reactions, reliably, across different partners. When you do this, they do that. Every time.

That reliability is the beginning of your personal game.

A game is not a style chosen from a menu. It is the set of positions, connections, and sequences that you have personally verified — through your own exploration — to produce consistent reactions that you know how to use. Nobody else has quite the same game, because nobody else has the same body, the same history, or the same map.

Practical implication: When a technique is not working for you, the question is not always "am I doing it wrong?" Sometimes the question is "does my body shape and timing actually suit this, or is there a variant that fits me better?" Exploration is how you find that out.

Sparring — The Integration Test

Sparring is what happens when you take everything you have explored and drilled — knee shield, DLR, your guard combinations, your funnel chains — and use them together against a fully resisting partner in real time. No pre-set sequences, no cooperative responses. Just two people, both trying to make their game work.

This is where everything gets tested. A technique that works in drilling can fail in sparring for reasons you would never discover any other way — timing, body mechanics, telegraphing, missing connections you did not know were important. Sparring reveals the gaps that drilling hides.

It also reveals what you genuinely own. If something works consistently in sparring — especially against people who know what you are going to do — it is yours.

What to look for in sparring

Not whether you won the round. Whether the thing you were working on showed up — and if it did not, where exactly it broke down. The breakdown point is the most useful information sparring can give you. Take it back to drilling, fix that specific thing, and test again.

Competition — The Proof-of-Concept Test

Competition is sparring with a stranger, under rules, with something at stake. That is the whole difference. The technical challenge is not greater than good sparring — in some ways it is less, because the opponent does not know your game at all.

What competition tests that sparring cannot is whether your game holds up when there is pressure you cannot control: an unfamiliar body type, an unexpected style, nerves, the clock. These are conditions you can approximate in the gym but never quite replicate.

This is why competition is called a proof-of-concept test. Not because winning is the proof — but because finding out whether your game works when everything is unknown and the stakes feel real is the most honest assessment available to you.

A note on perspective: Competing is not a requirement. It is an option. Some students find it useful as an accelerant — the preparation sharpens their game quickly. Others get equal benefit from consistent hard sparring with good training partners. What matters is that you are regularly testing your game against real resistance. Competition is one way to do that; it is not the only way.
