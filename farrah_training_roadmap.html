Phase 5 ▶ You Are Here Finishing & Technical Precision — D'Arce Choke Troubleshooting

Your most recent PT session (Session #90, 2 Apr 2026) worked specifically on a technique that kept failing — not because you don't know it, but because of a body mechanics error that was quietly undermining it:

  • D'Arce choke when partner turtles — two entry windows identified
  • Window 1 (mid-transition): as partner reaches for far knee — easier entry, one shoulder exposed. This worked well once reviewed.
  • Window 2 (full turtle): the error was attacking neck-to-armpit as default habit — this removed chest pressure and relied on arm strength alone. Partner could easily disconnect your arms.
  • Correction: thumb connecting to back of partner's neck for correct arm placement. Use your leg to counter the arm post while keeping chest-to-back connection → fall to the side → choke.
Key correction to carry: If your chest is not on their back, your arm strength will always be insufficient. The choke is powered by your body weight, not your arms. This applies to all upper body choke attacks from behind — not just the D'Arce.

Why these games matter for you specifically

Games 1–2: Single Limb — Leg then Arm
You will defend your hip line using one leg only, then one arm only. No other tools. This strips away all your technical knowledge and asks: what does your body do naturally? The answer tells you what habits are working and which need changing. This is directly connected to your guard work — your ability to stay in guard long enough to launch attacks like your armbar or D'Arce is exactly what is being trained here.
Game 3: One Leg + One Arm
You now combine both single-limb skills. The coordination challenge is real — you will start seeing how your leg and arm need to work together rather than competing for the same space. This has a direct transfer to your passing game too: when you are on top, this is exactly what your opponent is trying to do to you.
Games 4–5: Staggered Legs + Knees to Chest
Staggered legs teach you how one body shape covers multiple threat directions — the same geometry that makes your leg drag pass work on top. Knees-to-chest builds the defensive shell that becomes the restart position for your close guard attacks.
Game 6: Frame Recovery
When your legs are pushed away and your partner thinks they have passed — you use a frame arm to recover. This is the final loop: the habit of recovering rather than conceding. Directly relevant to re-establishing your guard before your opponent can consolidate the pass.
What to focus on today: The D'Arce correction from last session — chest pressure, not arm pressure — applies here too. When you use your arm as a frame today, feel the difference between pushing with your arm alone versus using your shoulder and body to create the frame. Same principle. Your whole game is becoming more body-weight-driven and less arm-driven.

Principles to Carry Into Every Session

Your Thinking Framework

Body weight beats arm strength — in every position.

The D'Arce correction from Session #90 was not just about that one choke. Every finishing position in your game works the same way: chest on back, shoulder on chest, hip weight on the frame. When something is not working, ask "am I using my body or just my arms?"

When you are playing guard: your job is to destabilise.

Get your partner's hips or hands to the mat. This is the trigger condition for all your attacks. Your close guard funnel, your scissors sweep, your omoplata — they all begin with destabilising, not with hunting a submission.

When something fails, the follow-up is already mapped.

You have the scissors sweep chain, the armbar funnel, and the D'Arce entry windows. You do not need to discover what to do next — it is already decided. The only question is: which response did they give? Answer that, and the next move is already chosen.

Understand the other side so you can beat it.

Session #89 taught you the knee shield from both top and bottom in the same class. This is intentional. Understanding what your opponent can do from each position tells you where the danger is and — more importantly — where the opportunity is. This is how you stop reacting and start anticipating.
