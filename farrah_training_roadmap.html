Phase 5 ▶ You Are Here Finishing & Technical Precision — D'Arce Choke Troubleshooting

Your most recent PT session (Session #90, 2 Apr 2026) worked specifically on a technique that kept failing — not because you don't know it, but because of a body mechanics error that was quietly undermining it:

  • D'Arce choke when partner turtles — two entry windows identified
  • Window 1 (mid-transition): as partner reaches for far knee — easier entry, one shoulder exposed. This worked well once reviewed.
  • Window 2 (full turtle): the error was attacking neck-to-armpit as default habit — this removed chest pressure and relied on arm strength alone. Partner could easily disconnect your arms.
  • Correction: thumb connecting to back of partner's neck for correct arm placement. Use your leg to counter the arm post while keeping chest-to-back connection → fall to the side → choke.
Key correction to carry: If your chest is not on their back, your arm strength will always be insufficient. The choke is powered by your body weight, not your arms. This applies to all upper body choke attacks from behind — not just the D'Arce.

The Pass Mechanics — What Was Difficult
The first hurdle: connecting your hips to your partner's lap and ratcheting your torso upward so your chest contacts their sacrum. From there you drive their hips over their shoulders and knees to the mat. The connection point — chest to sacrum — is what powers the pass. Without it you are pushing with your arms, which will not work against a resisting partner.
Game 1 — Getting Your Leg Under to Access the Far Arm
Starting at hip-to-hip, you sitting up and Daryl lying flat facing you. The task was to work out how to get your leg under his back to reach his far arm. This is the entry geometry — without the leg positioning, the crucifix arm trap cannot be established.
Game 2 — Leg Configuration to Secure the Far Arm
Once the entry was found, the second task was solving the exact leg shape that locks the far arm in place. The leg configuration does two things at once: traps the arm and severely limits how the partner can escape. Getting this wrong means the position is uncomfortable but not controlled.
Game 3 — Posture to Access the Submission Chain
With the trap established, the third task was the posture that keeps your partner from recovering while giving you access to the arms. From the crucifix, three triangles and three leg-only arm locks are available, alongside the most common finish — neck crank or straight arm lock. The submission you choose depends on what your partner exposes in trying to escape.
Three Common Escapes to Know
Partners most often try: (1) sliding down to remove the leverage, (2) freeing the trapped arm, or (3) turtling toward your head. Knowing these in advance means you are not surprised when they happen — each one creates a specific opportunity to finish or adjust your position.
The principle underneath all of it: chest to sacrum is not just the pass — it is the control mechanism for everything that follows. If that connection breaks, the partner recovers. Keep it, and the position stays yours. This is the same body-weight-over-arm-strength principle from your D'Arce work — it keeps reappearing because it is the foundation of how finishing positions work.

Principles to Carry Into Every Session

Your Thinking Framework

Body weight beats arm strength — in every position.

Chest to sacrum in the stack pass. Chest to back in the D'Arce. Shoulder to chest in the pin. The same principle keeps appearing. When something is not working, ask: am I using my body or just my arms?

When you are playing guard: your job is to destabilise.

Get your partner's hips or hands to the mat. This is the trigger condition for all your attacks. Your close guard funnel, your scissors sweep, your omoplata — they all begin with destabilising, not with hunting a submission.

When something fails, the follow-up is already mapped.

You have the scissors sweep chain, the armbar funnel, the D'Arce entry windows, and now three crucifix submission options each tied to a partner escape. You do not need to decide what to do — they tell you by what they expose.

Understand the other side so you can beat it.

You drilled the crucifix today as the attacker — Daryl was on the receiving end. That dual perspective is deliberate. Understanding how a position feels to be trapped in tells you exactly what pressure to maintain and which escape windows to close.
